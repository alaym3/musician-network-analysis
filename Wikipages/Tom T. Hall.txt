'''Thomas T. Hall''' (May 25, 1936 – August 20, 2021), nicknamed "the Storyteller",<ref name="WaPoObit">{{cite news |last1=Estrada |first1=Louie |title=Tom T. Hall, country music's hit-making 'Storyteller,' dies at 85 |url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/obituaries/tomt-hall-died/2021/08/21/fddbce08-0291-11ec-85f2-b871803f65e4_story.html |access-date=August 22, 2021 |work=Washington Post |date=August 22, 2021}}</ref> was an American [[country music]] singer-songwriter and short-story author. He wrote 12 No.&nbsp;1 hit songs, with 26 more that reached the Top 10, including the No.&nbsp;1 international [[pop (music)|pop]] [[Crossover (music)|crossover]] hit "[[Harper Valley PTA]]" and "[[I Love (Tom T. Hall song)|I Love]]", which reached No.&nbsp;12 on the [[Billboard Hot 100|''Billboard'' Hot 100]]. He is included in ''Rolling Stone''{{'}}s list of 100 Greatest Songwriters.

==Early life and career==
Hall was born in [[Olive Hill, Kentucky]], United States on May 25, 1936.<ref>{{cite web|title=Tom T. Hall, Country Music's 'Storyteller,' Is Dead at 85|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2021/08/21/arts/music/tom-t-hall-country-musics-storyteller-is-dead-at-85.html|last=Friskics-Warren|first=Bill|work=The New York Times|date=August 21, 2021|accessdate=August 21, 2021}}</ref><ref name="Larkin">{{cite book|title=[[Encyclopedia of Popular Music|The Virgin Encyclopedia of Popular Music]]|editor=Colin Larkin|editor-link=Colin Larkin (writer)|publisher=[[Virgin Books]]|date=1997|edition=Concise|isbn=1-85227-745-9|pages=561/2}}</ref> As a teenager, he organized a band called the Kentucky Travelers that performed before movies for a traveling theater.<ref name="Larkin"/> Hall enlisted in the [[United States Army|U.S. Army]] in 1957, serving in Germany.<ref name="nbcnews">[https://www.nbcnews.com/news/obituaries/tom-t-hall-country-music-storyteller-who-sang-about-life-n1277358 Tom T. Hall, country music storyteller who sang about life's simple joys, dies at 85] [[NBC News]]. Retrieved August 21, 2021.</ref><ref name="allmusic">[https://www.allmusic.com/artist/tom-t-hall-mn0000515628/biography Tom T. Hall; Biography by Stephen Thomas Erlewine] [[AllMusic]]. Retrieved August 21, 2021.</ref> While in the service, he performed over the [[Armed Forces Radio Network]] and wrote comic songs about army experiences.<ref name="Larkin"/> Following his discharge in 1961, he used [[G.I. Bill]] education benefits to enroll at [[Roanoke College]] where he worked as a disc jockey.<ref>{{cite web |title=Artists Spotlight {{!}} Roanoke College |url=https://www.roanoke.edu/about/175_anniversary/maroon_spotlight/artists_spotlight |website=www.roanoke.edu |access-date=January 23, 2020}}</ref> His early career included being an announcer at [[WRON (AM)|WRON]], a local radio station in [[Ronceverte, West Virginia|Ronceverte]], West Virginia. Hall was also an [[DJ|announcer]] at WMOR (1330 AM) in [[Morehead, Kentucky|Morehead]] and WGOH (1370 AM) in [[Grayson, Kentucky|Grayson]], both in Kentucky. Hall was also an announcer at WSPZ, which later became [[WVRC-FM|WVRC Radio]] in [[Spencer, West Virginia|Spencer]], West Virginia, in the 1960s.<ref>{{cite web|title=History of WSPZ/WVRC, Spencer |url=http://www.wvrcfm.com/Helping%20hands.html|publisher=WVRC|access-date=July 13, 2012}}</ref>

Hall's big songwriting break came in 1963, when country singer [[Jimmy C. Newman]] recorded his song "DJ For a Day".<ref name="Larkin"/> In 1964, he moved to [[Nashville, Tennessee|Nashville]] and started to work as a $50-a-week songwriter for Newkeys Music, the publishing company belonging to Newman and his business partner Jimmy Key, writing up to half a dozen country songs per day.<ref name="batey">{{cite web|last=Batey|first=Angus|url=https://www.theguardian.com/music/musicblog/2015/mar/31/cult-heroes-tom-t-hall-country-music-star-johnny-cash|title= Cult heroes: Tom T Hall, the singer who wrote of real lives and changing times|work=The Guardian|date=March 15, 2015|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> Key suggested that he add the middle initial "T" to his name.<ref name="batey"/> Hall was nicknamed "The Storyteller", and he composed songs for dozens of country music stars, including [[Johnny Cash]], [[George Jones]], [[Loretta Lynn]], [[Waylon Jennings]], [[Alan Jackson]], and [[Bobby Bare]]. He also penned "[[Hello Vietnam]]", a song that openly supported the [[Vietnam War]] at a time when war protest songs were beginning to dominate the pop music chart. The song proved to be a hit for country singer [[Johnnie Wright]] and was later used in the 1987 Vietnam War movie ''[[Full Metal Jacket]]''.<ref name=yahoo>{{cite news|last=Rossi|first=Rosemary|title=Tom T Hall, Country Singer Who Wrote 'Harper Valley PTA,' Dies at 85|url=https://news.yahoo.com/tom-t-hall-country-singer-172128699.html|publisher=[[Yahoo!]]|date=August 21, 2021|accessdate=August 22, 2021}}</ref>

One of his earliest successful songwriting ventures, "[[Harper Valley PTA]]", recorded in 1968 by [[Jeannie C. Riley]],<ref name="Larkin"/> hit No.&nbsp;1 on the [[Billboard Hot 100|''Billboard'' Hot 100]] and [[Hot Country Songs|Hot Country Singles]] charts a week apart. It sold over six million copies and won both a [[Grammy Award]] and [[Country Music Association Awards|CMA Award]]. The song would go on to inspire a motion picture and television program of the same name. Hall himself recorded the song for his album ''The Definitive Collection'' (as track No. 23). His recording career took off after Riley's rendition of the song, releasing a number of hits from the late 1960s through the early 1980s. Some of his biggest hits include "[[A Week in a Country Jail]]", "[[(Old Dogs, Children and) Watermelon Wine]]", "[[I Love (Tom T. Hall song)|I Love]]", "[[Country Is]]", "[[The Year Clayton Delaney Died]]", "[[I Like Beer]]", "[[Faster Horses (the Cowboy and the Poet)]]", and "[[That Song Is Driving Me Crazy]]".<ref name="Larkin"/> He is also noted for his child-oriented songs, including "Sneaky Snake" and "[[I Care (Tom T. Hall song)|I Care]]", the latter of which hit No.&nbsp;1 on the country charts in 1975. His song "[[I Love (Tom T. Hall song)|I Love]]", in which the narrator lists the things in life that he loves, was recorded by Heathen Dan, with completely altered lyrics, as "I Like"<ref>{{cite web|url=https://laughingsquid.com/i-like-heathen-dan-i-love-tom-t-hall/|title=A Disgusting Parody of the Lighthearted Classic 1973 Country Music Song 'I Love' by Tom T. Hall|author=Lori Dorn|publisher=Laughing Squid|date=July 16, 2018|accessdate=August 21, 2021}}</ref> and appeared many times on the [[Dr. Demento]] show in the early 1980s. Hall's song was also used with altered lyrics and a hard rock arrangement in a popular 2003 TV commercial for [[Coors Light]].<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2003/01/26/magazine/the-way-we-live-now-1-26-03-process-how-to-write-a-catchy-beer-ad.html|title=THE WAY WE LIVE NOW – 1-26-03 – PROCESS – How to Write a Catchy Beer Ad|date=January 26, 2003|work=The New York Times|access-date=January 23, 2016}}</ref> In the mid-to-late 1970s, Hall was a commercial spokesperson for [[Chevrolet]] trucks.<ref name=variety>{{cite news|last=Willman|first=Chris|title=Tom T. Hall, Country Hall of Famer Known for 'I Love' and 'Harper Valley PTA,' Dies at 85|url=https://variety.com/2021/music/obituaries-people-news/tom-t-hall-dead-country-music-legend-1235046057/|newspaper=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]|date=August 20, 2021|accessdate=August 22, 2021}}</ref>

Hall succeeded [[Ralph Emery]] as host of the [[Television syndication|syndicated]] country music TV show ''[[Pop! Goes the Country]]'' in 1980 and continued until the series ended in 1982.<ref>{{cite web|title=Full cast and crew for "Pop! Goes the Country"|url=https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0071035/fullcredits#cast|publisher=IMDb|access-date=July 13, 2012}}</ref> Hall largely retired from writing new material in 1986<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.oldies.com/artist-biography/Tom-T-Hall.html|title=Tom T. Hall Biography|website=Oldies.com|access-date=October 10, 2019}}</ref> and from performing in 1994;<ref name="countrystandardtime">{{Cite web|url=http://www.countrystandardtime.com/d/article.asp?xid=468|title=Tom T.Hall keeps a rappin' – October 1997|website=Countrystandardtime.com|access-date=October 10, 2019}}</ref> his last public performance, which was also his first in several years, was in 2011.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://theboot.com/tom-t-hall-dixie-hall-country-love-stories/|title=Tom T. + Dixie Hall – Country's Greatest Love Stories|first=Riane|last=Konc|website=The Boot|access-date=October 10, 2019}}</ref>

==Awards and honors==
Hall won the [[Grammy Award for Best Album Notes]] in 1973 for the notes he wrote for his album ''[[Tom T. Hall's Greatest Hits]]''. He was nominated for, but did not win, the same award in 1976 for his album ''[[Greatest Hits Vol. 2 (Tom T. Hall album)|Greatest Hits Volume 2]]''. He was a member of the [[Grand Ole Opry]] from 1971.<ref>{{cite web|title=Tom T. Hall |url=http://www.opry.com/artists/h/Hall_TomT.html|publisher=Grand Ole Opry|access-date=June 29, 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Opry Member List PDF |url=http://www.opry.com/img/Opry%20Members%20List.pdf|date=April 23, 2012|access-date=June 29, 2012 |url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120607030858/http://www.opry.com/img/Opry%20Members%20List.pdf|archive-date=June 7, 2012}}</ref> In 1998 his 1972 song "[[(Old Dogs, Children and) Watermelon Wine]]" came in second in a [[BBC Radio 2]] poll to find the UK's favorite [[easy listening]] record, despite never having been a hit in the UK and being familiar to Radio 2 listeners mostly through occasional plays by DJ [[Terry Wogan]].<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.highbeam.com/doc/1G1-60709394.html|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160220160859/https://www.highbeam.com/doc/1G1-60709394.html|url-status=dead|archive-date=February 20, 2016|title=Eagles' Hotel Flys to Top of Poll|work=Birmingham Post|date=December 8, 1998|page=16|access-date=January 23, 2016|via=Highbeam Research|url-access=subscription }}</ref>

Hall was inducted into the Kentucky Music Hall of Fame in 2002.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.kentuckymusichalloffame.com/induction/|title=KMHF Inductees|publisher=Kentucky Music Hall of Fame|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> On February 12, 2008, Hall was inducted into the [[Country Music Hall of Fame]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.bmi.com/news/entry/tom_t_hall_and_the_statler_brothers_join_the_country_music_hall_of_fame|title=Tom T. Hall and The Statler Brothers Join the Country Music Hall of Fame|publisher=Broadcast Music, Inc.|date=August 5, 2008|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> In regard to Hall's longer-than-anticipated wait to be inducted, he attributed it to being somewhat reclusive and "not well liked" among the [[Music Row|Nashville music industry]], noting that he almost never collaborated with other songwriters and by the 1990s was largely out of step with the corporate style of country music.<ref name="countrystandardtime"/> On June 1, 2014, ''[[Rolling Stone]]'' ranked "(Old Dogs, Children and) Watermelon Wine" at No. 93 on their list of the 100 greatest country songs.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.rollingstone.com/music/pictures/100-greatest-country-songs-of-all-time-20140601/93-tom-t-hall-old-dogs-children-and-watermelon-wine-1972-0157334|title=93. Tom T. Hall, 'Old Dogs, Children and Watermelon Wine' (1972)|work=Rolling Stone|date=June 2014|access-date=January 23, 2016}}</ref> In November 2018 Hall and his wife Dixie Hall were inducted into the International Bluegrass Music Hall of Fame.<ref>{{cite web|last=Kessler|first=K|url=https://ibma.org/?award_category=hall-of-fame-inductees/|title=Award Category: Hall of Fame Inductees|publisher=IBMA|date=November 14, 2018|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> On June 13, 2019, Hall was inducted into the [[Songwriters Hall of Fame]]. Of all the honors he had received in his lifetime, he considered this induction to be his proudest moment and the pinnacle of his achievement, also stating that he was taken by surprise for even being considered.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.cmt.com/news/1506920/in-the-words-of-tom-t-hall/|title = In the Words of Tom T. Hall}}</ref>

Together with his wife Dixie Hall he won the Society for the Preservation of Bluegrass Music of America Bluegrass Song Writer of the Year award in 2002,<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.spbgma.com/2002-music-awards-results--spbgma.html|title=2002 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> 2003,<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.spbgma.com/2003-music-awards-results--spbgma.html|title=2003 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> 2004,<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.spbgma.com/2004-music-awards-results--spbgma.html|title=2004 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> 2005,<ref>{{cite web|url= https://www.spbgma.com/2005-music-awards-results--spbgma.html|title=2005 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> 2007,<ref>{{cite web|url= https://www.spbgma.com/2007-music-awards-results--spbgma.html |title=2007 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> 2008,<ref>{{cite web|url= https://www.spbgma.com/2008-music-awards-results--spbgma.html|title=2008 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> 2009,<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.spbgma.com/2009-music-awards-results--spbgma.html
|title=2009 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> 2010,<ref>{{cite web|url= https://www.spbgma.com/2010-music-awards-results--spbgma.html |title=2010 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> 2011,<ref>{{cite web|url= https://www.spbgma.com/2011-music-awards-results--spbgma.html|title=2011 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> 2013,<ref>{{cite web|url= https://www.spbgma.com/2013-music-awards-results--spbgma.html|title=2013 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> 2014,<ref>{{cite web|url= https://www.spbgma.com/2014-music-awards-results--spbgma.html|title=2014 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref> and 2015.<ref>{{cite web|url= https://www.spbgma.com/2015-music-awards-results--spbgma.html |title=2015 Award Winners|publisher=spbgma.com|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Hall was married to Opal "Hootie" McKinney, a native of Grayson, Kentucky.<ref name="deanh">{{cite web|url=http://www.deanhall.com/tom-t-hall-son/|title=Tom T. Hall's Son?|date=August 21, 2016|access-date=January 12, 2019}}</ref><ref name="storyteller">{{cite book |last1=Hall |first1=Tom T. |title=The Storyteller's Nashville |date=October 2016 |publisher=Spring House Press |isbn=978-1-940611-44-0 |page=160}}</ref> Their son Dean Hall was born in 1961. Dean worked for his father in the early 1980s, first as a [[roadie]] and later as a guitarist. Dean has since worked as a solo artist and with [[Bobby Bare]]'s band.<ref name="deanh"/>

Hall met bluegrass songwriter Dixie Hall in 1965. Tom and Dixie met at a 1965 music industry award dinner she was invited to for having written (as Dixie Deen) the song "Truck Drivin' Son-of-a-Gun" which became a hit for [[Dave Dudley]].<ref name="alias"/> Dixie Hall was born Iris Lawrence in the [[West Midlands (region)|West Midlands, England]], in 1934 and emigrated to the U.S. in 1961. Hall and Dixie were married from 1968 until her death on January 16, 2015.<ref>{{cite web |last1=Whitaker |first1=Sterling |title=Songwriter Dixie Hall Dead at 80 |url=https://tasteofcountry.com/dixie-hall-dead-dies/ |website=Taste of Country |access-date=August 22, 2021 |language=en |date=January 17, 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |title=Dixie Hall, Songwriter and Wife of Tom T. Hall, Dead at 80 |url=http://www.cmt.com/news/1748137/dixie-hall-songwriter-and-wife-of-tom-t-hall-dead-at-80/ |website=CMT News |access-date=August 22, 2021 |date=January 17, 2015}}</ref><ref name="cooper">{{cite web|last=Cooper|first=Peter|url=https://tennessean.com/story/entertainment/music/2015/01/17/dixie-hall-prolific-bluegrass-songwriter-dies/21914181/|title= Dixie Hall, prolific bluegrass songwriter dies at 80 |work=The Tennessean|date=February 5, 2015|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref><ref name="alias">{{cite web|last=Himes|first=Jeffrey|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2008/01/13/arts/music/13hime.html|title= Who Needs Country Radio? Not Tom T. Hall|work=The New York Times|date=January 13, 2008|access-date=December 22, 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.discogs.com/artist/3916566-Dixie-Hall|title=Dixie Hall|publisher=discogs|access-date=December 23, 2018}}</ref> They lived in [[Franklin, Tennessee]].<ref name="cooper"/>

At age 85, Hall died at his home in [[Franklin, Tennessee]], on August 20, 2021.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.cnn.com/2021/08/20/entertainment/tom-t-hall-obit/index.html|title=Country Music Hall of Fame artist Tom T. Hall dies at age 85|first=Rebekah |last = Riess|website=CNN}}</ref>
