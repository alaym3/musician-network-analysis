'''Amos Lee''' (born '''Ryan Anthony Massaro''',<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.cas.sc.edu/engl/ugrad/alumni.html|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120531215518/http://www.cas.sc.edu/engl/ugrad/alumni.html |archive-date=May 31, 2012 |title=Alumni News|work=Department of English Language and Literature|publisher=University of South Carolina. cas.sc.edu|access-date=May 5, 2017}}</ref> June 20, 1977)<ref name="CreativeLoafing">{{cite web|url=http://charlotte.creativeloafing.com/gyrobase/who_is_amos_lee_/Content?oid=338842 |archive-url=https://archive.today/20130408002914/http://charlotte.creativeloafing.com/gyrobase/who_is_amos_lee_/Content?oid=338842 |url-status=dead |archive-date=April 8, 2013 |title=Who is Amos Lee? |last=Hahne |first=Jeff |date=July 30, 2008 |format=Interview |work=Creative Loafing |access-date=January 28, 2011 }}</ref> is an American singer-songwriter whose musical style encompasses [[folk music|folk]], [[rock music|rock]], and [[Soul music|soul]]. He was born in Philadelphia and graduated from the [[University of South Carolina]] with a degree in English. After working as a schoolteacher and bartender he began to pursue a career in music.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=https://eventseeker.com/artist/4729-amos-lee|title=Amos Lee - Artist Profile|website=eventseeker.com|language=en|access-date=2019-12-20}}</ref> His manager Bill Eib, an artist manager and new artist development agent, submitted a demo recording to [[Blue Note Records]] which resulted in a recording contract and association with singer [[Norah Jones]].<ref name=":0" />

Since that time Lee has recorded five albums on Blue Note Records and has toured as an opening act for [[Norah Jones]], [[Bob Dylan]], [[Elvis Costello]], [[Paul Simon]], [[Merle Haggard]], [[Van Morrison]], [[John Prine]], [[Dave Matthews Band]], [[Adele]], the [[Zac Brown Band]], [[Jack Johnson (musician)|Jack Johnson]], [[The Avett Brothers]], and [[David Gray (musician)|David Gray]]. His music has appeared on the soundtracks of numerous TV shows and movies. He has performed on several late night TV shows and at a voter registration rally for [[Barack Obama]]. In 2011, his album ''[[Mission Bell (Amos Lee album)|Mission Bell]]'' debuted at No. 1 on the [[Billboard 200|''Billboard'' 200]] chart.<ref name="Globe and Mail"/><ref name="NPR Music 2"/><ref name="nytimes.com"/>

==Early life and education==
Lee was born Ryan Anthony Massaro in 1977 and was raised in [[Kensington, Philadelphia]].<ref name="Philly News">Hiltbrand, David (March 21, 2011) [http://articles.philly.com/2011-03-27/news/29194919_1_amos-lee-vibes-previous-three-albums/2 Amos Lee: Philadelphia's anonymous hit-maker] ''Inquirer/Philly News''</ref><ref name="Daily News 2005"/> He moved to [[Cherry Hill, New Jersey]] at age 11 and graduated from [[Cherry Hill High School East]].<ref name="NPR Music 2"/><ref name="nytimes.com"/><ref name="Daily News 2005"/> Lee attended the [[University of South Carolina]] and graduated with a degree in English and a minor in education.<ref name = "Boston Globe Feb 2005"/> During his college years, he developed an interest in music after being inspired by ''[[Great Days: The John Prine Anthology]]''.<ref name="nytimes.com"/><ref name = "Boston Globe Feb 2005"/> During this period, he began playing the guitar and bass as part of a band and listening to the music of [[Donny Hathaway]], [[Joni Mitchell]], [[Luther Vandross]], [[Bill Withers]], and [[Otis Redding]].<ref name = "Boston Globe Feb 2005"/><ref name="gitlin">{{cite news|last=Gitlin|first=Lauren|url=https://www.rollingstone.com/artists/amoslee/articles/story/7092465/10_artists_to_watch_amos_lee|title=10 Artists to Watch: Amos Lee|work=Rolling Stone|date=March 10, 2005|access-date=August 24, 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080222093307/http://www.rollingstone.com/artists/amoslee/articles/story/7092465/10_artists_to_watch_amos_lee|archive-date=February 22, 2008|url-status=dead}}</ref><ref name="No Bells">Lindquist, David (October 21, 2005) No bells or whistles; Amos Lee, on his first tour as a headliner, aims to serve the song above all,  ''The Indianapolis Star'' page=34</ref>

==Career==
After returning to Philadelphia, Lee worked as a second grade teacher at the Mary McLeod Bethune School and as a bartender at local music venues. He performed at open mic events in the area and, through his manager Bill Eib's contacts with promoters, was hired as an opening act for [[Mose Allison]] and [[B.B. King]].<ref name="Daily News 2005"/>

In 2003, Lee's manager Bill Eib sent a four-song demo CD to several record labels, and the representative at [[Blue Note Records]] was "immediately struck by his [Lee's] voice".<ref name = "Boston Globe Feb 2005">Graham, Renee (February 27, 2005)  "Former Teacher Gets a Lesson in the Big Time", ''The Boston Globe'' page N5</ref> Afterwards, [[Norah Jones]] heard Lee's music while visiting the record company and invited Lee to be the opening act for her 2004 tour.<ref name="nytimes.com">{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2006/10/04/arts/music/04amos.html?scp=2&sq=amos+lee&st=nyt|title=A Ready-Made Idol? Nope, Just a Folkie Happy to Play His Music|author=Winter, Miller|work=The New York Times|date=October 4, 2006|access-date=December 3, 2011}}</ref>

The friendship between Lee's manager Bill Eib and [[Bob Dylan]]'s manager Jeff Kramer resulted in Lee touring with Dylan as his opening act in early 2005.<ref name="Daily News 2005"/><ref>(June 26, 2005) Watch this Face: Amos Lee,  ''Independent on Sunday'' (London) page 15</ref><ref name="PBS News">PBS New Hour, Mission Bell Amos Lee enriches his songwriting with star power, April 28, 2011. Saskia De Melker, Retrieved July 2011</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=4541649|title=Philadelphian Amos Lee's Country-Fried Soul|date=March 18, 2005|publisher=NPR Music|access-date=July 9, 2011}}</ref> Later, Lee began touring on his own and recorded his self-titled and "widely praised" debut album of "subtle, folky soul" produced by [[Norah Jones]]' bassist, [[Lee Alexander (musician)|Lee Alexander]]<ref name="Philly News"/><ref name="No Bells"/><ref>(March 4, 2005) The Ticket; CD Reviews, ''The Irish Times'', page 13</ref><ref name="NPR Music">{{cite web|url=https://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=6286383|title=A Moment of Peace on the Long Road|date=October 18, 2006|publisher=NPR Music|author=Blaustein, Claire|access-date=July 9, 2011}}</ref> which included vocals and instrumentation by Norah Jones and members of her band.<ref name=":0" /><ref name = "Boston Globe Feb 2005"/> After it was released, the album peaked at No. 2 on the ''[[Billboard (magazine)|Billboard]]'' [[Top Heatseekers]] chart and Lee was named one of ''[[Rolling Stone]]'''s "Top 10 Artists to Watch."<ref name="gitlin"/><ref>(June 10, 2006) Top Heatseekers, ''[[Billboard (magazine)]]''</ref> One song from the album, called "Colors", appeared on the TV show ''[[Grey's Anatomy]]'' and in the film ''[[Just Like Heaven (film)|Just Like Heaven]]''.<ref name="USA Today"/> Lee's music received additional media attention when he performed on late-night TV shows such as the ''[[Late Show with David Letterman]]''<ref>Green, Andy (February 17, 2011) [https://web.archive.org/web/20110221150415/http://www.rollingstone.com/culture/blogs/rolling-stone-video-blog/unlikely-billboard-champion-amos-lee-performs-new-song-violin-on-letterman-20110217 Unlikely Billboard Champion Amos Lee Performs New Song, Violin, On 'Letterman] ''Rolling Stone'' Retrieved December 3, 2011</ref> and ''[[The Tonight Show with Jay Leno]]''.<ref>{{cite news| title=TV highlights: Shark Week, and 'The Bachelorette' finale | work=The Washington Post | date=Aug 5, 2013 | author=Yahr, Emily}}</ref>

In 2006, Lee recorded his second album, ''[[Supply and Demand (Amos Lee album)|Supply and Demand]]'', which was produced by a friend of manager Bill Eib, Philadelphia musician and producer Barrie Maguire. An [[NPR Music]] reviewer described it as having "more complicated instrumentation and production" than his prior work.<ref name="NPR Music"/> The song "Shout Out Loud" was released as a single and peaked at No. 76 on the [[Billboard 200|''Billboard'' 200]],<ref>{{cite web|url={{BillboardURLbyName|artist=amos lee|chart=all}}|title=Supply and Demand, Amos Lee|publisher=Prometheus Global Media|work=Billboard|access-date=December 2, 2011}}</ref> and another song, called "Sweet Pea", was used in an [[AT&T]] ad campaign.<ref name="USA Today">{{cite web|url=https://www.usatoday.com/life/music/2011-02-20-amos-lee-otv_N.htm|title=On the verge: Amos Lee goes from chalkboard to 'Billboard'|date=February 25, 2011|author=Mansfield, Brian|work=USA Today|access-date=July 9, 2011}}</ref>

Lee's third studio album, ''[[Last Days at the Lodge]]'' was released in 2008 and re-emphasized "his grounding in folk and soul". The album peaked at number 29 on the ''Billboard'' 200 chart and Lee performed at the Change Rocks [[voter registration]] rally for [[Barack Obama]] in [[Philadelphia]] that summer opening for [[Bruce Springsteen]].<ref name="NPR Music 2">{{cite web|url=https://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=92691367|title=Amos Lee At Studio 4A 'Lodge'|date=July 19, 2008|publisher=NPR Music|access-date=July 9, 2011}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url={{BillboardURLbyName|artist=amos lee|chart=all}}|title=Amos Lee: Last Days At The Lodge|publisher=Prometheus Global Media|work=Billboard|access-date=December 2, 2011}}</ref><ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.kimmelcenter.org/news/index.php?id=227 | title=Philly Native Singer-Songwriter Amos Lee Performs at the Merriam Theater | publisher=[[Kimmel Center for the Performing Arts]] | date=December 21, 2010 | access-date=April 30, 2011}}</ref>

In 2011, Lee released his fourth album on [[Blue Note Records]], entitled ''[[Mission Bell (Amos Lee album)|Mission Bell]]'' which was produced by [[Joey Burns]] of [[Calexico (band)|Calexico]]. The album debuted at number one on the ''Billboard'' 200 chart, number one on the [[Digital Albums]] chart, number two on the [[Internet]] chart, and number one on the [[Amazon (company)|Amazon]] Top-Selling Albums and [[iTunes]] charts.<ref>Burger, David (February 2, 2011)  Amos Lee's new album debuts at No. 1, ''The Salt Lake Tribune'', Retrieved July 9, 2011</ref> The album's single, "Windows are Rolled Down", became a top 10 hit on ''[[USA Today]]''{{'}}s [[adult album alternative|adult-alternative]] chart.<ref name="USA Today"/> However, the album also has the dubious distinction of being the lowest-selling, number one ''Billboard'' album as of 2011 selling only 40,000 copies.<ref name="Wash Po 2013">{{cite news | title=Wale hits No. 1 with 'The Gifted' (Posted 2013-07-04 01:21:36); D.C. rapper debuts in the top spot on the Billboard albums chart | work=Washington Post | date=July 4, 2013 | author=Richards, Chris}}</ref> Guest artists on the album included [[Lucinda Williams]], [[Willie Nelson]], [[Priscilla Ahn]], [[Pieta Brown]], [[James Gadson]], and [[Sam Beam]].<ref name="PBS News"/><ref>{{cite web | url=http://thekey.xpn.org/2010/09/amos-lee-gets-cozy-with-indie-rockers-folk-heros-and-country-legends/ | title=Amos Lee gets cozy with indie rockers, folk heros, and country legends | year=2010 | access-date=September 9, 2010 |work=The Key| archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20100911042617/http://thekey.xpn.org/2010/09/amos-lee-gets-cozy-with-indie-rockers-folk-heros-and-country-legends/| archive-date= September 11, 2010 | url-status= live}}</ref>

Lee appeared at [[Farm Aid]] 2013  and on the compilation album, ''[[The Music Is You: A Tribute to John Denver]]''.<ref>{{cite news | title=Win: John Denver tribute album | publisher=Milton Keynes Citizen | date=March 22, 2013 | author=Staff writer}}</ref> He released his fifth studio album, ''[[Mountains Of Sorrow, Rivers Of Song]]'' in October 2013.<ref>Poulsen, Drew (June 5, 2013) [http://www.billboard.com/articles/news/1565722/amos-lee-sets-date-for-mountains-of-sorrow-rivers-of-song-album amos-lee-sets-date-for-mountains-of-sorrow-rivers-of-song-album] Billboard, accessed Aug 6, 2013</ref><ref>{{cite news|author=Staff writer|title=Farm Aid 2013 Is Sold Out|date=July 18, 2013|publisher=Agriculture Week}}</ref>

==Reception==
Lee's "folksy, bluesy sound" has been compared to that of [[John Prine]] and [[Norah Jones]].<ref>{{cite news|title=Spanning decades; Amos Lee's music draws two generation|author=Darvas, Lori|work=The Indianapolis Star|date=August 8, 2008|location=Indiana|page=21}}</ref> His music is said to utilize the "supple funk of his vocals and arid strum of his guitar" while recalling "the low-volume, early-'70s acoustic soul of stars like [[Bill Withers]] and [[Minnie Ripperton]]".<ref name="Daily News 2005">{{cite news|title=He Follows a Different Strummer|author=Farber, Jim|work=Daily News|date=March 1, 2005|location=New York|page=38}}</ref> A ''[[The New York Times|New York Times]]'' music critic described Lee as having a "honeyed singing voice – light amber, mildly sweet, a touch of grain" which he features "squarely, without much fuss or undue strain" in his "1970s folk rock and rustic soul" musical song craft.<ref name="NY Times">{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2011/01/25/arts/music/25choice.html|title=Critics' Choice: New CDs|work=The New York Times|author1=Ben Ratliff|author2=Chinen, Nate|author3=Pareles, Jon|date=January 24, 2011|access-date=July 9, 2011}}</ref> According to a music writer at [[ABC News]], Lee "has that folksy, bluesy vibe, with a bit of country twang" and a voice that is "ever soulful".<ref name="ABC">{{cite news|url=https://abcnews.go.com/Entertainment/wireStory?id=12812348|title=Review: Amos Lee Spirit Breaks, but Songs Fly|date=February 1, 2001|work=ABC News|author=Moody, Nekesa|access-date=July 9, 2011}}</ref> Simultaneously Lee has been both lauded and dismissed as the "male Norah Jones" and<ref name="Globe and Mail">{{cite news|title=Taking a back seat to the songs|author=Wheeler, Brad|work=Globe and Mail |location=Canada |date=November 3, 2006|page=25}}</ref> his lyrics are said to convey "the complexities of everyday emotions" without falling into flowery imagery.<ref name = "Boston Globe Feb 2005"/> Lee's songs have appeared on a number of TV shows including ''[[House (TV series)|House]]'' and ''[[Parenthood (2010 TV series)|Parenthood]]''.<ref name="USA Today"/><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.parenthoodtvsoundtrack.com/ |title=Parenthood Soundtrack |publisher=Parenthoodtvsoundtrack.com |access-date=December 1, 2011 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20111211174850/http://www.parenthoodtvsoundtrack.com/ |archive-date=December 11, 2011 }}</ref>

==Personal life==
Amos Lee describes himself as being of [[multiracial people|mixed heritage]]; he admits he is not fully aware of his background.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://hamptonroads.com/2006/10/amos-lee%2526%2523039%3Bs-background:-touchy-subject|title=Amos Lee's Background: Touchy Subject|work=Hampton Roads|date=October 17, 2006|access-date=December 2, 2011|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121026215417/http://hamptonroads.com/2006/10/amos-lee%2526%2523039%3Bs-background:-touchy-subject|archive-date=October 26, 2012|url-status=dead}}</ref> He maintains a residence in [[West Philadelphia]].

He took the stage name "Amos Lee" because he got sick of people mispronouncing his real name.<ref>{{cite podcast |url=https://maximumfun.org/episodes/depresh-mode/amos-lee-gets-deep-gets-dark-makes-jokes/ |title=Amos Lee Gets Deep, Gets Dark, Makes Jokes |website=Depresh Mode |publisher=[[Maximum Fun]] |host=[[John Moe]] |date=October 18, 2021 |access-date=October 19, 2021}}</ref>
