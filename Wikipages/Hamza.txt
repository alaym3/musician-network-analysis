'''Hamza''' ({{lang-ar|Ù‡Ù…Ø²Ø©}}, ''{{transl|ar|ALA|hamzah}}'') ({{lang|ar|[[wikt:Ø¡|Ø¡]]}}) is a letter in the [[Arabic alphabet]], representing the [[glottal stop]] {{IPAblink|Ê”}}. Hamza is not one of the 28 "full" letters and owes its existence to historical inconsistencies in the [[orthography|standard writing system]]. It is derived from the Arabic letter ''[[Ayin|Ê¿Ayn]]'' ({{lang|ar|[[Ø¹]]}}). In the [[Phoenician alphabet|Phoenician]] and [[Aramaic alphabet|Aramaic]] alphabets, from which the Arabic alphabet is descended, the glottal stop was expressed by ''[[aleph|alif]]'' ({{lang|phn|ğ¤€}}), continued by ''Alif'' (&nbsp;{{lang|ar|Ø§}}&nbsp;) in the Arabic alphabet. However, Alif was used to express both a glottal stop and also a long vowel {{IPA|/aË/}}. In order to indicate that a glottal stop is used, and not a mere vowel, it was added to Alif [[diacritic]]ally. In modern orthography,  hamza may also appear on the line, under certain circumstances as though it were a full letter, independent of an Alif.

== Etymology ==
''Hamza'' is derived from the verb ''{{transl|ar|ALA|hamaza}}'' ({{lang-ar|Ù‡ÙÙ…ÙØ²Ù}}) meaning 'to prick, goad, drive' or 'to provide (a letter or word) with hamzah'.<ref>{{cite book|last=Wehr|first= Hans|editor-last= Cowan|editor-first= J. M.|title= The Hans Wehr Dictionary of Modern Arabic|publisher= Otto Harrassowitz KG|year= 1994|chapter= Ù‡Ù…Ø² ''hamaza''|isbn=978-0-87950-003-0|edition=4th}}</ref>

== Arabic "seat" rules ==

The rules for writing hamza differ somewhat between languages even if the writing is based on the [[Arabic abjad]]. The following addresses Arabic specifically.

=== Summary ===
* '''Initial hamza''' is always placed over (Ø£ for {{transl|ar|ALA|Ê¾a-}} or {{transl|ar|ALA|Ê¾u-}}) or under (Ø¥ for {{transl|ar|ALA|Ê¾i-}}) an alif.
* '''Medial hamza''' will have a seat or be written alone:
** Surrounding vowels determine the seat of the hamza with preceding long vowels and diphthongs (such as {{transl|ar|ALA|aw}} or {{transl|ar|ALA|ay}}) being ignored.
** ''{{transl|ar|ALA|i-}}'' (Ø¦) over ''{{transl|ar|ALA|u-}}'' (Ø¤) over ''{{transl|ar|ALA|a-}}'' (Ø£) if there are two conflicting vowels that count; on the line (Ø¡) if there are none.
** As a special case, ''{{transl|ar|ALA|ÄÊ¾a, Å«Ê¾a}}'' and ''{{transl|ar|ALA|awÊ¾a}}'' require hamza on the line, instead of over an alif as one would expect. (See III.1b below.)
* '''Final hamza''' will have a seat or be written alone:
** Alone on the line when preceded by a long vowel or final consonant.
** Has a seat matching the final short vowel for words ending in a short vowel.
* ''Two adjacent alifs are never allowed.'' If the rules call for this, replace the combination by a single [[Aleph#Alif maddah: Ø¢|alif maddah]].

=== Detailed description ===
*Logically, hamza is just like any other letter, but it may be written in different ways.  It has no effect on the way other letters are written.  In particular, surrounding long vowels are written just as they always are, regardless of the "seat" of the hamzaâ€”even if this results in the appearance of two consecutive wÄws or yÄÊ¾s.
*Hamza can be written in five ways: on its own ("on the line"), under an alif, or over an alif, wÄw, or yÄÊ¾, called the "seat" of the hamza. When written over yÄÊ¾, the dots that would normally be written underneath are omitted.
*When according to the rules below, a hamza with an alif seat would occur before an alif which represents the vowel ''Ä'', a single alif is instead written with the maddah symbol over it.
*The rules for hamza depend on whether it occurs as the initial, middle, or final letter (not sound) in a word. (Thus, final short inflectional vowels do not count, but ''{{transl|ar|ALA|-an}}'' is written as alif + [[nunation]], counts, and the hamza is considered medial.)

I. If the hamza is initial:

*If the following letter is a short vowel, ''{{transl|ar|ALA|[[Arabic diacritics|fatá¸¥ah]]}}'' (a) (as in <big>{{lang|ar|Ø£ÙÙÙ’Ø±ÙØ§Ø¯}}</big> ''{{transl|ar|ALA|Ê¾afrÄd}}'') or ''{{transl|ar|ALA|á¸ammah}}'' (u) (as in <big>{{lang|ar|Ø£ÙØµÙÙˆÙ„}}</big> ''{{transl|ar|ALA|Ê¾uá¹£Å«l}}''), the hamza is written over a place-holding alif; ''{{transl|ar|ALA|kasrah}}'' (i) (as in <big>{{lang|ar|Ø¥ÙØ³Ù’Ù„ÙØ§Ù…}}</big> ''{{transl|ar|ALA|Ê¾islÄm}}'') the hamza is written under a place-holding alif and is called "hamza on a wall."

II. If the hamza is final:

*If a short vowel precedes, the hamza is written over the letter (''{{transl|ar|ALA|alif, wÄw,}}'' or ''{{transl|ar|ALA|yÄÊ¾}}'') corresponding to the short vowel.
*Otherwise, the hamza is written on the line (as in <big>{{lang|ar|Ø´ÙÙŠÙ’Ø¡}}</big> ''{{transl|ar|ALA|shayÊ¾&nbsp;}}'' "thing").

III. If the hamza is medial:

*If a long vowel or diphthong precedes, the seat of the hamza is determined mostly by what follows:

:*If ''{{transl|ar|ALA|i}}'' or ''{{transl|ar|ALA|u}}'' follows, the hamza is written over ''{{transl|ar|ALA|yÄÊ¾}}'' or {{transl|ar|ALA|wÄw}}, accordingly.
:*Otherwise, the hamza would be written on the line. If a ''{{transl|ar|ALA|yÄÊ¾}}'' precedes, however, that would conflict with the stroke joining the ''{{transl|ar|ALA|yÄÊ¾}}'' to the following letter, so the hamza is written over ''{{transl|ar|ALA|yÄÊ¾}}.'' (as in <big>{{lang|ar|Ø¬ÙØ¦ÙØª}}</big>)
*Otherwise, both preceding and following vowels have an effect on the hamza.

:*If there is only one vowel (or two of the same kind), that vowel determines the seat (''{{transl|ar|ALA|alif, wÄw,}}'' or ''{{transl|ar|ALA|yÄÊ¾}}'').
:*If there are two conflicting vowels, ''{{transl|ar|ALA|i}}'' takes precedence over ''{{transl|ar|ALA|u}}'', ''{{transl|ar|ALA|u}}'' over ''{{transl|ar|ALA|a}}'' so ''{{transl|ar|ALA|miÊ¾ah}}'' 'hundred' is written <big>{{lang|ar|Ù…ÙØ¦ÙØ©}}</big>, with hamza over the ''{{transl|ar|ALA|yÄÊ¾}}.''

== Hamza in other Arabic-based scripts ==

=== Urdu/Shahmukhi script ===
In [[Urdu]] script, hamza does not occur at the initial position over alif since alif is not used as a glottal stop in Urdu. In the middle position, if hamza is surrounded by vowels, it indicates a [[diphthong]] between the two vowels. In the middle position, if hamza is surrounded by only one vowel, it takes the sound of that vowel. In the final position hamza is silent or produces a glottal sound, as in Arabic.

In Urdu, hamza usually represents a diphthong between two vowels. It rarely acts like the Arabic hamza except in a few loanwords from Arabic.

Hamza is also added at the last letter of the first word of [[ezÄfe]] compound to represent -e- if the first word ends with [[Yodh|yeh]] or with [[He (letter)|he]] or over [[Yodh|bari yeh]] if it is added at the end of the first word of the ezÄfe compound.

Hamza is always written on the line in the middle position unless in [[Waw (letter)|waw]] if that letter is preceded by a non-joiner letter; then, it is seated above waw. Hamza is also seated when written above bari yeh. In the final form, Hamza is written in its full form. In ezÄfe, hamza is seated above he, yeh or bari yeh of the first word to represent the -e- of ezÄfe compound.

=== Uyghur script ===
In the [[Uyghur Arabic alphabet]] the hamza is not a distinct letter and is not generally used to denote the glottal stop, but rather to indicate vowels. The hamza is only depicted with vowels in their initial or isolated forms, and only then when the vowel starts a word. It is also occasionally used when a word has two vowels in a row.<ref>{{cite book |last1=Nazarova |first1=Gulnisa |last2=Niyaz |first2=Kurban |title=Uyghur: An Elementary Textbook |date=December 2013 |publisher=Georgetown University Press |location=Washington D.C. |isbn=9781589016842 |pages=5â€“8 |edition=Bilingual}}</ref>
